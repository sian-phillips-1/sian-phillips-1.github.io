Australian Wildfires

The aim: To visualise data on the Australian wildfires and incorporate it with national park spatial data to highlight the desructive nature of the fires.

The Process

The wildfires of the 2019-2020 bush fire season in Australia were some of the worst on record, impacting 19 million hectares of land and destroy ing the habitats of over 3 billion native animals. This damage occurred in many of Australiaâ€™s Protected areas, which cover over 22.1% of the country and contain significant ecological landscapes. The visualisation examines the relationship between wildfires and protected areas during this time

My interest was drawn to the landscape affected by the wildfires and I wanted to understand what type of land was being burned. The data on protected areas gave me this information as it provided insight into the significant environmental regions across Australia. These regions are often protected for important reasons, as they can contain valuable ecosystems and habitats of endangered species.

The intention of the visualisation was to communicate the reality of the effect of wildfires on the natural environment. I wanted to add context to the devastating impact that the wildfires had and continue to have in Australia. Most people have an understanding of what a protected area is, they associate it with beautiful landscapes or endangered species. I wanted to use this pre-existing association to create an emotional response and connection to the data. To achieve this fully, I intended to highlight specific areas that were affected and display the number of wildfires occurred over the one-month time-period. This would place the large numbers in context and on a smaller scale, making the reality easier to grasp. The intention was to not be overly aggressive with the message but use the visualisation to guide readers to reach their own conclusion. Most importantly, I wanted to tell a story about the real environmental impact, not just an aesthetically pleasing representation of wildfire coordinates. Therefore, it was key for the design choices not to embellish the information but to try and encode it in an honest and precise manner.

R was used for all the plotting. The packages used were:

  • dplyr: for cleaning the data.
  • maps: for the geographical map of Australia.
  • mapdata: for the zoomed in region of Australia.
  • sf: for encoding spacial vector data of the wildfires and protected land regions.
