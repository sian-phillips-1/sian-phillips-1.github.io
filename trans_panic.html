Trans Panic

The aim: to use data visualisations to explain the key components of transgender moral panic in the UK. The project looks to provide a rational and evidence-based approach to a complex societal issue. The data visualisations are designed to enhance engagement and understanding of trans panic. By challenging attitudes and practices that continue to drive the panic it is the hope that society can move towards a more equitable and compassionate approach to gender identity and expression.



The Process

Transgender issues have become increasingly prevalent in, the news media, political debates and public discussion. This heightened coverage and attention has framed transgender people as a threat to moral norms and has induced a sense of panic surrounding related gender issues. This study uses data visualisation along with Stanley Cohen’s framework of ‘moral panic’ to investigate and explain trans panic in the UK. Through the investigation it was observed that trends in the news media, public opinion, policy, and transgender people’s experiences, align with those typical of a moral panic. In relation to transgender issues; media coverage has been increasing, the public have become less accepting, policies designed to support legal gender recognition have been blocked and transgender people are facing rising levels of harassment, along with other negative side effects. By utilising the method of data visualisation, this project aims to speak to a broad audience, promoting better understanding of a highly relevant social issue in a clear and concise manner.


All the graphs were created in R using a combination of Base-R and ggplot2. The BeautifulSoup package in Python was used to scrape online articles. To analyse the language used in news article headlines and extract the most common words, the package collections was used. Some of the basic charts can be seen in the image above. These were exported from R into Figma where the colours, fonts and designs were tweaked to fit with the style of the final visualisation piece.

The pages on the left, used in the final design, were made during a workshop at the very start of the project when brainstorming for ideas. The strong colours of the red, black and orange created a boldness that I wanted to carry over into my final project. I was also drawn to the idea of making imprints with leaves, symbolising a sense of lasting-impact and permanence. In these leaves, and throughout nature are repeating patterns, often made sense of by the Fibonacci Sequence and the Golden Ratio. This inspired me to connect the patterns of nature with my visualisation about patterns in society.
